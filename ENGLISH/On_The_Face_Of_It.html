Flamingo (Play) – "On The Face Of It" by Susan Hill

SHORT SUMMARY

"On The Face Of It" is a touching play that explores themes of isolation, acceptance, and inner strength. It follows the interaction between Derry, a young boy with a burnt face, and Mr. Lamb, an elderly man with a physical disability. Derry is self-conscious about his appearance and avoids people, while Mr. Lamb teaches him the importance of self-acceptance and embracing life despite physical imperfections. The play highlights the struggles of people with disabilities and the need for social inclusion and understanding.

Theme

Isolation and Loneliness

The play emphasizes how physical disabilities can lead to emotional isolation and social neglect.

Acceptance and Courage

Mr. Lamb encourages Derry to accept himself and face the world with confidence.

Overcoming Prejudices

Society's harsh judgment towards physical differences is critiqued through Derry's experiences.

Important Questions

What is Derry's main struggle in the play?

Derry struggles with self-acceptance due to his burnt face and society's negative perception of him.

How does Mr. Lamb help Derry?

Mr. Lamb teaches Derry to embrace life, ignore people's judgments, and find joy in simple things.

What message does the play convey?

The play promotes self-acceptance, overcoming prejudices, and seeing beyond physical appearances.

Key Quotes

"Things look different when you look at them properly."

"It’s all relative. Beauty and the beast."

"You should not be afraid of people’s remarks; they don’t count."

Literary Devices

Symbolism

Mr. Lamb’s garden symbolizes hope, new beginnings, and acceptance.

Irony

Despite his wisdom, Mr. Lamb is lonely, just like Derry.

Contrast

Derry’s pessimism contrasts with Mr. Lamb’s optimism.

THE END
