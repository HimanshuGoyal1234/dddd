Chapter 8: Physiology and Sports

1. Physiological Factors Determining Components of Physical Fitness

Physical fitness is influenced by various physiological factors:

1.1 Strength

Factors affecting strength:

  • Muscle size (cross-sectional area)
  • Muscle composition (fast-twitch vs slow-twitch fibers)
  • Neuromuscular coordination
  • Age and gender
  • Hormonal factors (testosterone, growth hormone)

1.2 Speed

Physiological determinants of speed:

  • Muscle fiber type (percentage of fast-twitch fibers)
  • Muscle elasticity
  • Energy systems (ATP-PC system efficiency)
  • Reaction time
  • Body composition

1.3 Endurance

Factors affecting endurance:

  • Cardiovascular efficiency (heart size, stroke volume)
  • Respiratory efficiency (lung capacity, VO2 max)
  • Muscle capillary density
  • Mitochondrial density in muscle cells
  • Glycogen storage capacity

2. Effects of Exercise on Muscular System

Short-term Effects

  • Increased muscle temperature
  • Increased muscle pliability
  • Muscle fatigue due to lactic acid accumulation
  • Micro-tears in muscle fibers (during intense exercise)

Long-term Effects

  • Hypertrophy (increase in muscle size)
  • Increased myoglobin content
  • Increased mitochondria number
  • Improved neuromuscular coordination
  • Increased glycogen storage capacity
  • Improved lactate tolerance

3. Effects of Exercise on Cardio-Respiratory System

Cardiovascular Effects

  • Short-term: Increased heart rate, stroke volume, cardiac output
  • Long-term: Cardiac hypertrophy (athlete's heart), lower resting heart rate, increased blood volume

Respiratory Effects

  • Short-term: Increased breathing rate, tidal volume
  • Long-term: Increased vital capacity, improved gas exchange, increased respiratory endurance

VO2 Max: Maximum amount of oxygen an individual can utilize during intense exercise. It's a key indicator of cardiovascular fitness.

4. Physiological Changes Due to Aging

System Changes Due to Aging Exercise Benefits
Muscular System Loss of muscle mass (sarcopenia), decreased strength, reduced flexibility Slows muscle loss, maintains strength, improves flexibility
Cardiovascular System Decreased cardiac output, reduced elasticity of arteries, higher blood pressure Maintains cardiac function, improves circulation, lowers blood pressure
Respiratory System Reduced lung capacity, weaker respiratory muscles Maintains lung function, improves breathing efficiency
Skeletal System Bone density loss (osteoporosis), joint stiffness Slows bone loss, maintains joint mobility
Nervous System Slower reaction time, reduced coordination Maintains neural connections, improves balance

5. Gender Differences in Physical Performance

Physiological Differences

  • Body composition (higher fat percentage in females)
  • Muscle mass (males typically have more muscle mass)
  • Bone structure (wider pelvis in females)
  • Cardiovascular capacity (higher VO2 max in males)
  • Hormonal differences (testosterone vs estrogen effects)

Performance Implications

  • Strength and power activities show significant gender differences
  • Endurance activities show smaller differences
  • Flexibility is generally greater in females
  • Recovery rates may differ due to hormonal variations

6. Ergogenic Aids in Sports

Types of Ergogenic Aids

  • Mechanical: Special equipment, clothing
  • Psychological: Hypnosis, meditation
  • Physiological: Blood doping, oxygen supplementation
  • Pharmacological: Drugs, steroids
  • Nutritional: Supplements, sports drinks

Dangers of Performance Enhancing Drugs

  • Cardiovascular problems
  • Liver damage
  • Hormonal imbalances
  • Psychological dependence
  • Ethical and legal consequences

7. Training Adaptations in Different Sports

Endurance Sports (e.g., Marathon)

  • Increased mitochondrial density
  • Enhanced fat metabolism
  • Improved lactate threshold
  • Cardiac hypertrophy (especially left ventricle)

Power Sports (e.g., Weightlifting)

  • Muscle hypertrophy
  • Increased fast-twitch fiber development
  • Enhanced neuromuscular coordination
  • Improved ATP-PC system efficiency

Team Sports (e.g., Basketball, Football)

  • Mixed aerobic and anaerobic adaptations
  • Improved recovery between bursts of activity
  • Enhanced agility and coordination
  • Sport-specific skill development

Key Terms and Definitions

Hypertrophy: Increase in size of muscle fibers due to exercise training.

Atrophy: Decrease in size of muscle fibers due to inactivity.

Stroke Volume: Amount of blood pumped by the left ventricle in one contraction.

Cardiac Output: Volume of blood pumped by the heart per minute (Stroke Volume Ã— Heart Rate).

Sarcopenia: Age-related loss of muscle mass and strength.
