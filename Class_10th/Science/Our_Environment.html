Our Environment - Class 10 Science

Introduction

The environment is the sum total of everything around an organism including both living and nonliving components. The living and nonliving factors influence the environment.

Components of the Environment

Abiotic Components

  • Climatic factors: wind, water, light, and temperature.
  • Physiographic factors: height, slope.
  • Edaphic factors: minerals, soil, texture, soil organisms, pH of the soil.

Biotic Components

  • Grazing animals
  • Insects
  • Lichens
  • Humans
  • Parasites

Ecosystem

An ecosystem is a structural and functional unit of the biosphere comprising living organisms and their interaction with food chains and biogeochemical cycles.

Types of Ecosystem

  • Natural Ecosystem: lakes, ponds, forests.
  • Artificial Ecosystem: garden, crop field, aquarium.

Organisms in the Ecosystem

  • Producers: Organisms that make their own food by utilizing solar energy, e.g., green plants, phytoplanktons.
  • Consumers: Organisms that depend on producers for their nutritional requirements.
    • Herbivores: Feed on green plants, e.g., deer, goat.
    • Carnivores: Feed on other animals, e.g., tiger, lion.
    • Omnivores: Feed on both plants and animals, e.g., humans.
    • Parasites: Live on or inside other organisms (host) for nutrition, e.g., lice.
  • Decomposers: Organisms that feed on dead and decaying organic matter, e.g., bacteria, fungi.

Food Chain

A food chain is a sequence where one organism eats another. It includes producers, consumers, and decomposers. Each step is a trophic level where energy transfer occurs.

Examples of Food Chains

  • Terrestrial Food Chain: Green Plants → Deer → Lion
  • Aquatic Food Chain: Aquatic Plants → Zooplankton → Small Fish → Large Fish

Food Web

A food web is an interconnected network of food chains where any organism can be eaten by several others.

Energy Transfer in the Food Chain

Only 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is transferred to the next. This is known as the 10% law. The remaining energy is lost as heat.

Biological Magnification

The increase in the concentration of harmful substances at each trophic level is called biological magnification. This occurs because these substances accumulate in the bodies of organisms and increase in concentration as they move up the food chain.

Ozone Depletion

Ozone (O₃) in the stratosphere protects the Earth from harmful UV radiation. The thinning of the ozone layer, known as ozone depletion, is caused by synthetic chemicals like Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).

Effects of Ozone Depletion

  • Skin cancer
  • Eye disorders
  • Global warming

Montreal Protocol

An international agreement made in 1987 to freeze the production of CFCs and promote the use of alternative sources to protect the ozone layer.

Management of Garbage

Methods for disposing of waste include:

  • Landfills
  • Composting
  • Incineration
  • Biogas and manure production

Environmental Pollution

Pollution is an undesirable change in the physical, chemical, and biological characteristics of air, water, and soil, leading to health problems and ecological imbalance.

Conclusion

Understanding our environment and the impact of human activities is crucial for sustainable living and preserving the Earth's ecosystems.
