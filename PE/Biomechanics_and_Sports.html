Chapter: Biomechanics and Sports

1. Introduction to Biomechanics

Biomechanics: The study of the mechanical laws relating to the movement or structure of living organisms, especially athletes in sports contexts.

1.1 Importance in Sports

  • Improves athletic performance through movement optimization
  • Helps prevent injuries by analyzing stress points
  • Enhances equipment design (shoes, rackets, etc.)
  • Develops better training techniques
  • Analyzes and improves sports techniques

Areas of Biomechanics

Kinematics ➔ Kinetics ➔ Sports Equipment Design ➔ Injury Prevention

2. Kinematics in Sports

2.1 Types of Motion

Type Description Sports Example
Linear Movement in straight line 100m sprint, bobsled
Angular Rotation about an axis Gymnastics pirouette, discus throw
General Combination of linear and angular Long jump (run + takeoff), basketball layup

2.2 Kinematic Variables

Displacement (Δx) = Final Position - Initial Position

Velocity (v) = Δx / Δt

Acceleration (a) = Δv / Δt

Projectile Motion Factors

Angle of Release ➔ Speed of Release ➔ Height of Release ➔ Air Resistance

3. Kinetics in Sports

Newton's Laws of Motion
  1. Law of Inertia: A body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a force (e.g., sprinter in starting blocks)
  2. Law of Acceleration: F = ma (e.g., golf club applying force to ball)
  3. Law of Reaction: For every action, equal and opposite reaction (e.g., swimmer pushing against water)

3.1 Types of Forces in Sports

  • Muscular Force: Generated by muscle contraction
  • Gravitational Force: Acts downward on all objects
  • Frictional Force: Opposes motion between surfaces
  • Air Resistance: Opposes motion through air
  • Ground Reaction Force: Surface pushing back on athlete

3.2 Momentum and Impulse

Momentum (p) = mass × velocity

Impulse (J) = force × time = change in momentum

Applications: Catching a cricket ball (increasing time to reduce force), golf swing (maximizing time of force application)

4. Levers in Human Body

Lever: A rigid bar that rotates about a fulcrum when force is applied, used to overcome resistance.

4.1 Classes of Levers

Class Components Order Example in Body Mechanical Advantage
First Fulcrum between effort and resistance Head nodding (atlanto-occipital joint) Can be >1 or <1< /td>
Second Resistance between fulcrum and effort Standing on toes (calf raise) Always >1
Third Effort between fulcrum and resistance Biceps curl Always <1< /td>

Lever System Components

Fulcrum (Joint) ➔ Effort (Muscle Force) ➔ Resistance (Weight/External Force)

5. Equilibrium and Stability

5.1 Types of Equilibrium

  • Static: Body at rest (e.g., handstand)
  • Dynamic: Body moving with constant velocity (e.g., cycling at steady speed)

5.2 Factors Affecting Stability

  1. Base of Support: Larger base increases stability (e.g., wrestler's stance)
  2. Center of Gravity: Lower COG increases stability (e.g., squat position)
  3. Mass: Greater mass increases stability (e.g., sumo wrestlers)
  4. Line of Gravity: Should be within base of support for stability

Stability Continuum

High Stability (e.g., Sumo stance) ↔ Low Stability (e.g., Gymnast on beam)

7. Technology in Biomechanics

7.1 Analysis Tools

  • Motion Capture Systems: High-speed cameras with markers
  • Force Platforms: Measure ground reaction forces
  • EMG: Electromyography for muscle activity
  • Pressure Mapping: Foot pressure distribution
  • Wind Tunnels: Aerodynamic testing

7.2 Applications

Key Terms and Formulas

Center of Gravity: The point where the entire weight of the body may be considered to act.

Work (W) = Force × Displacement × cosθ

Power (P) = Work / Time

Torque (τ) = Force × Lever Arm

Angular Momentum: L = Iω (Moment of Inertia × Angular Velocity) - conserved in mid-air movements.

Drag Force: FD = ½ρv2CDA - affects projectiles and athletes moving through air/water.
