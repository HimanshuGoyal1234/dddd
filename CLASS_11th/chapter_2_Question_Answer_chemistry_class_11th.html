Why can't the spin quantum number have a value of -¬Ω for the first electron in an orbital?
Understanding Spin Quantum Number

The spin quantum number (s) describes the intrinsic angular momentum of an electron.

It can have only two possible values: +¬Ω or -¬Ω.

Pauli Exclusion Principle

The first electron in an orbital can have either spin, but by convention, we assign it +¬Ω.

The second electron in the same orbital must have -¬Ω to satisfy the Pauli Exclusion Principle.

Final Answer: It's a convention to assign +¬Ω first, but both spins are physically possible.

Which quantum numbers define a specific orbital in an atom?
Unique Identification of Orbitals

An orbital is uniquely defined by three quantum numbers:

  1. Principal quantum number (n)
  2. Azimuthal quantum number (l)
  3. Magnetic quantum number (m)
Why Not Spin?

The spin quantum number (s) describes the electron, not the orbital itself.

Two electrons can occupy the same orbital (same n, l, m) but must have opposite spins.

Final Answer: Orbitals are defined by n, l, and m.
