Life Processes – Class 10 Biology

What are Life Processes?

Life processes are the basic activities that are essential for an organism to stay alive. These include:

  • Nutrition
  • Respiration
  • Transportation
  • Excretion
  • Reproduction
  • Growth
  • Movement
  • Control and Coordination
  • Homeostasis

1. Nutrition

Nutrition is the process of taking in food and converting it into energy and body substances.

Types of Nutrition:

  • Autotrophic Nutrition: Organisms make their own food (e.g., plants).
  • Heterotrophic Nutrition: Organisms depend on others for food (e.g., animals).

Photosynthesis (in plants):

Carbon dioxide + Water + Sunlight → Glucose + Oxygen

Occurs in the presence of chlorophyll (green pigment).

2. Respiration

Respiration is the process of breaking down food to release energy.

Types of Respiration:

  • Aerobic: With oxygen, produces more energy (in humans, most animals).
  • Anaerobic: Without oxygen, less energy (in yeast, some bacteria).

Equation: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy (ATP)

3. Transportation

Transportation refers to the movement of substances like nutrients, gases, and wastes within organisms.

In Human Beings:

  • Heart: Pumps blood through the body.
  • Blood: Transports oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste.
  • Arteries: Carry blood away from the heart.
  • Veins: Carry blood toward the heart.

In Plants:

  • Xylem: Transports water and minerals.
  • Phloem: Transports food from leaves to rest of plant.

4. Excretion

Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste products from the body.

In Humans:

  • Kidneys: Filter blood to form urine.
  • Ureters, bladder, urethra: Form the urinary system.

In Plants:

Waste is removed by falling leaves, excretion through stomata, and storage in vacuoles.

5. Reproduction

Reproduction is the process by which organisms produce offspring.

Types of Reproduction:

  • Asexual Reproduction: Single parent, offspring are genetically identical (e.g., binary fission in bacteria).
  • Sexual Reproduction: Two parents, offspring are genetically diverse (e.g., humans, most animals).

6. Growth

Growth is the increase in size and number of cells.

Types of Growth:

  • Cellular Growth: Increase in cell size.
  • Cell Division: Increase in cell number.

Growth is influenced by factors like nutrition, hormones, and environment.

7. Movement

Movement is the change in position of an organism or its parts.

Types of Movement:

  • Locomotion: Movement of the entire organism (e.g., walking, swimming).
  • Internal Movement: Movement within the organism (e.g., blood circulation, peristalsis).

8. Control and Coordination

Control and coordination are the mechanisms that regulate and integrate body functions.

In Animals:

  • Nervous System: Controls voluntary and involuntary actions.
  • Endocrine System: Regulates body functions through hormones.

In Plants:

  • Hormones: Regulate growth, development, and responses to the environment.

9. Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the maintenance of internal stability despite external changes.

Examples of Homeostasis:

  • Thermoregulation: Maintaining body temperature.
  • Osmoregulation: Maintaining water and salt balance.

Key Points to Remember

  • Chlorophyll is essential for photosynthesis.
  • ATP is the energy currency of the cell.
  • Blood has plasma, RBCs, WBCs, and platelets.
  • Nephrons are the filtering units in kidneys.
  • Reproduction can be asexual or sexual.
  • Growth is influenced by nutrition, hormones, and environment.
  • Movement includes locomotion and internal movement.
  • Control and coordination involve the nervous and endocrine systems.
  • Homeostasis maintains internal stability.
